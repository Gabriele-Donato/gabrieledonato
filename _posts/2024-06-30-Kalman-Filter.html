Filtering is a statistical technique used to extract information from a noisy signal. The core idea of this methodology is that the observations are produced by a vector of hidden states. While the observations are independent from each other, the hidden states follow a first-order Markov process (i.e. the state at time depends only on the state at time ).

 

As an example consider the following: it is a pleasant day in the city park and you amuse yourself observing people walking along a fairly distant and tree-lined trail which is perfectly perpendicular to your position. In particular, you fix your attention on a certain person. At time you observe him at point , then he walks behind a bush and you try to estimate where he will be at the next time step. Having formed a rough idea of his velocity, you expect to see him appear at point . However, when time finally comes, you see him at point , then he disappears again  behind a large tree. Leveraging on the information at the preceding step, you modify your beliefs and refine your prediction for time .

Forecasting Amazon stock prices

In order to put into practice what has been derived thus far, consider a time-series of Amazon stock prices. Lets consider the adjusted closing price, i.e. the closing price accounting for dividends, stock splits and stock offerings.

 

The price at the current time will be modeled using three states: the price at the previous time, the velocity at which the price increases, and its acceleration. This modeling approach has been chosen since kinematics does not include any causal hypothesis: it models movement in a purely geometric fashion. It is the underlying, a-cyclical structure of the present model that affords a causal interpretation, so that through this choice it will be possible to understand the benefits of the current approach.

The only thing that remains to be defined is the measurement noise . In the present application,  it will be a scalar since the only measurement is the price.

 

Note that measurement noise, along with the variance associated with the acceleration, are assigned arbitrarily. While a low value for the measurement noise reflects confidence in the reliability of the data-source, a low variance for the acceleration implies confidence in the predictions. With higher acceleration variance the model trusts measurements more than predictions; with lower values for the acceleration variance, the model trusts more the predictions.

 

It should be noted, as remarked above that the noise is not modeled directly but through the variance and covariances implied by the distributions.
