I casually encountered the concept of statistical entropy while skimming through the textbook for a course on general statistics that I was attending at my university [3]. The simple and elegant axiomatic derivation presented thereby exerted on me a whimsical fascination: it was coupled with intriguing terms such as “information” and “surprise,” appealing for someone, like me, whose background was mainly humanistic.

 

Advancing in my studies, I could find applications of entropy in unexpected ways: for instance, in determining  prior distributions in a Bayesian setting when information is scarce, or in the Kullback-Leibler divergence, useful to measure the distance between two data distributions.

 

Although my textbook spent barely half-a-page to introduce the concept, and it does not really require more, I believe that it should deserve a more comprehensive and disambiguating introduction.

 

As recalled by Claude Shannon, the father of “information theory,” with reference to the discussions with his friend and renowned mathematician John Von Neumann:

 

“Von Neumann told me, ‘You should call it entropy, for two reasons. In the first place your uncertainty function has been used in statistical mechanics under that name. In the second place, and more importantly, no one knows what entropy really is, so in a debate you will always have the advantage.’” [2]

 

Therefore, it is appropriate to commence one’s inquiry from the field of physics, and precisely from the original problems and meaning that the concept of entropy came to acquire in statistical mechanics.

 

French Thermal Machines

 

Late 19th century. The changes produced by the steam engine in European economies and societies were so dramatic that the period will become known as the “industrial revolution.” Designing efficient machines was an economic and political objective more than a scientific one: for a country like France this would have meant overcoming the competitions of powerful neighbours: Germany and Great Britain [4].

 

Partly motivated by these reasons, a French mathematician by the name of Lazare Carnot had started exploring the problem. In his Fundamental Principles of Equilibrium and Movement (1803), he had reached the conclusion that the accelerations of the moving parts of the engines all entailed losses of useful work (i.e. that it was not possible to convert all of the energy produced by combustion into the movement of the pistons of the engine.)

 

In Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire, Sadi, Lazare’s son, further elaborated on his father’s discoveries through a mental experiment of an idealised engine in which thermodynamic processes could be reversed, assuming the absence of friction and functional imperfections.

 

The ideal “thermal machine” of Sadi Carnot, is characterised by a four-phased cycle: two expansions and two compressions, in both cases one isothermal and one adiabatic.[1] 

 

In practice, a Carnot Engine consists of a cylinder containing a piston on which a certain weight (e.g. sand) is placed. The gas below the piston is expanded through the exposure to a certain temperature ,  and the progressive removal of the weight on the piston makes it move up (isothermal expansion). The weight is progressively removed and the piston continues its ascent, however the temperature decreases as the system, which does not share heat with the environment, enters in a phase of adiabatic expansion. At this point, a second source of heat (colder than the first) and the progressive addition of weight on the piston cause it, through two new phases, one isothermal and one adiabatic, to compress the gas: volume, temperature and pressure return to the starting levels (reversibility).

Entropic Cups of Tea

 

The second principle of thermodynamics derives from the studies presented above, and it is known in multiple formulations. One possible statement is attributed to British physicist William Thomson (also known as Lord Kelvin): it is not possible to devise a transformation whose unique result is the absorption of heat from a single source in order to transform it into work. In plainer terms, for the piston to keep working it is necessary to cede some heat to a colder source.

 

Another possible, and equivalent statement of the law, attributed to German physicist Rudolf Clausius, is that it is not possible to realise a transformation whose unique result is the transfer of heat from a colder to a hotter body. The classic examples that illustrate the consequences of this principle are the fridge or the air conditioner: these machines transfer heat from colder to hotter bodies, however this is not a contradiction of the law since these processes are counterbalanced by an increased consumption of heat (i.e. the inversion in the heat transfer is not the unique result of the transformation.)

 

Clausius is famously known for having introduced the concept of entropy as a description of the energy  losses to which a thermodynamic system is bound. In this sense, the second law of thermodynamics can be enunciated as follows:

 

In a closed system entropy tends to a maximum.

 

The above definition is particularly useful, since it clearly implies e.g. the impossibility of perpetual motion machines: if entropy tends to a maximum, a system that exchanges energy but not matter with the environment (closed), is bound to dissipate all of its energy. In this sense entropy might be interpreted as a measure of disorder of a system.

 

As an example, consider mixing tea with milk: at first the milk creates hypnotising shapes, however, as entropy increases, the liquids mix and a novel and homogeneous configuration, in which nothing seems to happen emerges. This is precisely what a probabilistic understanding of entropy is really about: there are so many more combinations for the particles of milk to spread out in the tea and create a homogeneous mix, than combinations of particles in which the milk remains in beautiful and pleasant shapes; so many more that the probability of consistently observing states in which the particles produce those wondrous curls is highly improbable. To be sure, while it seems that nothing is happening at the macro-level, at the micro-level it’s a total mess: there are countless events and configurations taking place.

 

Consider also how many times the wired headphones, stored in a pocket, are found to be knotted in impossible ways: there are many more ways for the headphones to get knotted than to remain orderly packet. Entropy is everywhere.

 

Multiverses and Time Arrows 

 

Energy seems to be spreading more and more, as heat flows through a privilege path from hotter to colder bodies and not all the way around. This implies that the arrow of time is going in one direction: forward. Once the egg is broken, it is not possible to decrease its entropy and restore it to the original state, except through an impossibly achievable increase in entropy elsewhere.

 

Projecting these intuitions into distant futures, some researchers have introduced the concept of heat-death of the universe. If the universe was a closed system, there would arrive a time in which entropy had reached a maximum and nothing notable would happen anymore anywhere at a macroscopic  level (similarly to what happens in the cup of milk and tea).

 

Time asymmetry is one of the current puzzles to be solved in cosmology. The point is that we can explain why entropy will be higher tomorrow, but not why it was lower yesterday. While this is not the focus of the present article, it seems that entropy contributes to the theory of multiverses. Statistically, given the sheer number of universes in the latter scenario, it would be more probable to witness universes being born in low entropy states, and evolving into higher entropy states. In such a multiverse, there would be a symmetric time arrow implying that for some universes time runs backwards. When compared to us, the past of the people inhabiting those distant universes would be our future, however the comparison is hypothetical because neither of us could travel between universes [1].

 

Axiomatic Surprises

 

The concept of entropy remained for a long time into the precincts of physics, until a brilliant researcher working at Bell Laboratories decided that the time was ripe to solve the fundamental problem of communication, i.e. the transmission of a message produced in a certain location to another location. Our hero went by the name of Claude Shannon, and he is acknowledged as the father of “information theory.” His ideas were breakthroughs because for the first time he had conceptualised messages as realisations of a stochastic process, and defined the engineering aspect of the problem as distinct from the semantics of the message [2].

 

This is the crucial intersection, where concepts become blurry and words such as “surprise”,“information” and “data compression” mix in confusing ways. Nonetheless, the axiomatic derivation eases most of the understanding effort, and therefore it is the natural place to start.

Conclusion

 

I hope that this post was able to clarify how the meaning of entropy, a concept that   Is found in different fields with strikingly similar functional forms, changed over time.

 

The fascination with words such as “information” as a form of “intelligence” has attracted also physicists such as Leó Szilárd, trying to solve the Maxwell demon problem [2].

 

The truth is that nobody really knows what entropy is in general, while it remains unquestionably a valuable tool in statistical analysis.

[1] Isothermal comes from the Greek iso-, same, and thermos, temperature. Adiabatic comes from a-, without, dia-, through, batos, passable, “that cannot be passed through.” Isothermal processes are those in which a transfer of heat is required, and the variation of temperature remains constant (e.g. boiling some water.) On the other hand, a process is said to be adiabatic when no transfer of heat occurs, but a change in the temperature due to an internal variation of the system is registered. Notably, in both transformations energy and temperature vary in the same direction, since they are directly proportional. However, in adiabatic transformations energy and work vary in opposite direction: think of a pump for bicycle wheels, it gets hot as we pump the wheel but no heat source is introduced in the system (adiabatic compression, associated with negative work and increasing energy in the system); or, think of the jet of a spry, it feels cold to touch because the expansion is so rapid that the jet fails to absorb the heat of the environment (adiabatic expansion, associated with positive work and decreasing energy in the system.)
